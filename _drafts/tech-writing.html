Organise the Reusable Content

It may be tempting to organise your Global project with a folder for each project and a folder for ‚Äòcommon‚Äô content. It makes sense in terms of what the global project represents, but it means that a writer needs to have knowledge of what is in each project. Without that knowledge, how can they:

  1. Determine which folder contains the snippets they want to use?
  2. Know whether a snippet is only reused in one manual?
  3. Know whether a snippet is reused in several manuals and so is a ‚Äòcommon‚Äô snippet?
  4. Know that content in a new project already exists in one of the other projects?

A better approach is to organise the content based on the type and meaning of the content.

The important thing: make sure the sub-folders are meaningful and don‚Äôt require the writer to already know the content. If they are looking for reusable steps, they start with the Process Steps folder, and then look for the step(s) that they want. They don‚Äôt need any prior knowledge of what is in each of the projects.

The images above show two different approaches ‚Äì on the left, the content is organised by type and then subject; on the right, it is organised by subject and then type.

Organising your global project in this way also helps to future-proof your project. If you have to add a new project, it is likely that its content will fit into this structure. If it doesn‚Äôt fit this structure, you can add folders to allow for the differences, again based on type and meaning.

Tip:¬†If you find that there are too many levels of information, try to remove one of the upper levels of folders. The lower levels are usually closely related to the meaning of the content, which is the most important part.

Tip:¬†To avoid confusion between writers (and possibly translators too), keep your conditions simple. Try to limit conditions to just the different output types and projects (or product versions).
